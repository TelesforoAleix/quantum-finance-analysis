## Abstract summary
The paper examines how big data technologies are transforming financial risk management, with emphasis on predictive modelling, real-time risk assessment, and the associated implementation challenges. It reviews the use of machine learning and streaming analytics for tasks such as credit risk assessment, fraud detection, and liquidity and market risk prediction, and contrasts these approaches with traditional risk methods. The study also discusses emerging issues around data quality, integration, model interpretability, regulation, cybersecurity, and skills gaps, and outlines future directions involving quantum computing, blockchain, explainable AI, and alternative data sources in financial risk management.
## Methodology
The paper is a peer-reviewed theoretical/narrative synthesis of the role of big data in financial risk management rather than an original empirical or formal quantum study. Its approach is conceptual and literature-based: it organizes prior work into major themes including predictive modeling, real-time risk assessment, emerging implementation challenges, and future technology trends. The paper develops a descriptive framework contrasting traditional versus big-data-driven risk management, classifies machine learning model types by financial use case, and outlines a high-level system architecture for real-time data ingestion and processing using streaming and cloud technologies. It also includes illustrative case examples and prescriptive mitigation strategies for issues such as data quality, interpretability, cybersecurity, compliance, and talent constraints. Quantum computing is discussed only as a future trend and strategic possibility for optimization, pricing, and machine learning acceleration, drawing on external sources such as BIS analyses, but no formal quantum model, theorem, proof, or proposition is developed. The paper’s implicit assumptions are that larger, faster, and more diverse data combined with ML/AI and streaming infrastructure can improve risk prediction and operational resilience, provided governance, explainability, and regulatory controls are in place.

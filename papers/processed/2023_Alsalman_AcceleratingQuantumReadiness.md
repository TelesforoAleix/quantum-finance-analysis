## Abstract summary
The paper discusses how organizations across sectors can prepare strategically for the emergence of quantum computing, with a focus on risk management, governance, and readiness planning. It outlines benefits of quantum readiness such as competitiveness, security, and data structuring, and evaluates different approaches including establishing in-house quantum offices, contracting with quantum companies, and leveraging national and international quantum organizations. The author also proposes governmental standardization and a dedicated Quantum Computing Standards organization to coordinate best practices and certification for quantum readiness globally.
## Methodology
The paper is a peer-reviewed theoretical/conceptual article that develops a governance- and risk-management-oriented framework for organizational quantum computing readiness rather than presenting formal mathematical models or empirical experiments. Its approach is narrative and prescriptive: it synthesizes prior literature, industry reports, organizational examples, and standards discussions to argue for early sectoral preparation for quantum computing. The article structures its analysis around (1) the need for readiness, (2) expected benefits such as competitiveness, security, and structured data, (3) proposed readiness pathways including establishing an internal quantum computing office, contracting with major quantum technology firms, and learning from national quantum organizations, and (4) policy recommendations such as governmental standardization and creation of an international Quantum Computing Standards organization (QCS). The underlying theoretical framing is strategic risk management and governance, with assumptions that quantum computing will materially affect sectors, create cybersecurity risks, and require proactive institutional capability-building. The paper advances practical propositions and recommendations rather than formal proofs, deriving them from comparative discussion of examples such as NEOM-Arqit, Honeywell/JPMorgan, national quantum initiatives, and Responsible Research and Innovation (RRI) as a guiding governance principle.

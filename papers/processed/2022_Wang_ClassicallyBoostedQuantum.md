## Abstract summary
The paper introduces the Classically-Boosted Quantum Optimization Algorithm (CBQOA), a hybrid quantum-classical method for combinatorial optimization that first uses a classical algorithm to find an approximate solution and then applies a continuous-time quantum walk-based circuit to explore its neighborhood for better feasible solutions. The approach is designed to handle both unconstrained and many constrained problems (e.g., Max 3SAT, Max Bisection, Maximum Independent Set, Portfolio Optimization) without penalty terms, while keeping the quantum state evolution within the feasible subspace and avoiding the need for efficient indexing of feasible solutions. The author details the graph and circuit constructions, parameter optimization strategy, and presents empirical results showing that CBQOA can outperform classical baselines and GM-QAOA on selected benchmark instances.
## Methodology
This paper is a preprint and presents a hybrid quantum-classical optimization method, the Classically-Boosted Quantum Optimization Algorithm (CBQOA), for combinatorial optimization. The approach first runs a classical approximation algorithm to generate a feasible seed solution, then constructs a weighted graph over feasible solutions and prepares an initial quantum state by a continuous-time quantum walk (CTQW) starting from that seed. The CBQOA ansatz then alternates phase separators based on the problem cost Hamiltonian with generalized reflections about the CTQW-prepared initial state, using variational parameters optimized by minimizing Conditional Value at Risk (CVaR). The paper develops the formal graph construction and CTQW implementation under structural assumptions on feasible-solution permutations, and demonstrates the method empirically on Max 3SAT and Max Bisection. For Max 3SAT, the seed is generated by the Karloff-Zwick SDP-based approximation algorithm with random hyperplane rounding; for Max Bisection, the seed is generated by the Feige-Langberg SDP-based approximation algorithm with randomized projection/rounding. Performance is evaluated through classical simulation on benchmark sets of hard random instances, comparing CBQOA against the classical seed generators and GM-QAOA using probability-of-good-solutions metrics at specified approximation-ratio thresholds.

**Algorithms used:** CBQOA, CTQW, GM-QAOA, ADAM, Karloff-Zwick algorithm, Feige-Langberg algorithm, SDP relaxation, random hyperplane rounding, RPR2 randomized rounding
**Frameworks:** Orquestra

**Experimental setup:** All results were obtained via classical simulation rather than on real quantum hardware. CBQOA was simulated for Max 3SAT and Max Bisection. For Max 3SAT, the CTQW was implemented exactly. For Max Bisection, the CTQW was implemented approximately using Trotterization with N = 3. Parameter optimization for both CTQW parameters (t, theta) and ansatz parameters (beta, gamma) used CVaR minimization with confidence level alpha = 0.5 and the ADAM optimizer. Simulations used an acceleration technique from Appendix A with discretization parameter M = 1000. Experiments were run on the Orquestra platform.

**Dataset:** Synthetic benchmark instances of combinatorial optimization problems rather than financial datasets. The study used 100 hard random Max 3SAT instances and 100 hard random Max Bisection instances. Although portfolio optimization is discussed as a target application in the theory section, no portfolio dataset or financial market data were used in the experiments.
## Experiment details
### Input
Two synthetic instance sets were generated. Max 3SAT: 100 hard instances, each with 16 variables and 200 clauses; clause weights were sampled independently and uniformly from [0,1]. Hardness filtering: for each candidate instance, the Karloff-Zwick SDP was solved and random hyperplane rounding was repeated 10,000 times; the instance was retained if the estimated fraction of solutions with approximation ratio >= 0.7 was below 0.05. Max Bisection: 100 hard instances generated from Erdős-Rényi random graphs G(n=12, p=0.5); edge weights were sampled independently and uniformly from [-1,1]. Hardness filtering: for each candidate graph, the Feige-Langberg SDP was solved and the RPR2 rounding procedure was repeated 10,000 times; the instance was retained if the estimated fraction of bisections with approximation ratio >= 0.99 was below 0.05.

### Process
For each Max 3SAT instance, the Karloff-Zwick SDP-based approximation algorithm was used to generate a seed assignment. A weighted hypercube CTQW was then constructed, with edge weights defined by a sigmoid of local cost differences around the seed. The CTQW parameters t and theta were optimized by minimizing CVaR at alpha = 0.5. The resulting state served as the initial state for CBQOA, after which p-layer circuits alternating phase separators exp(-i gamma H_f) and reflections about the initial state exp(-i beta |psi><psi|) were optimized using ADAM. For Max Bisection, the Feige-Langberg SDP plus RPR2 rounding generated the seed bisection. A CTQW over feasible bisections was approximated by Trotterized XY-type gates with N = 3, again with t and theta optimized via CVaR minimization. Then p-layer CBQOA circuits were optimized similarly. The paper evaluated CBQOA at depths p = 0,1,2,3 and compared especially CBQOA_0 and CBQOA_3 against GM-QAOA_3 and repeated classical baselines (e.g., KZ^10, FL^5). During simulation, an approximation method discretizing the cost spectrum into M = 1000 bins was used to accelerate CVaR estimation.

### Output
The main outputs were histograms and comparative performance statistics based on probability of good solutions (POGS), defined as the probability that an algorithm returns a solution whose approximation ratio exceeds a threshold. For Max 3SAT, the reported thresholds included 0.7 and 0.8; for Max Bisection, the threshold was 0.99. Comparisons were made among repeated classical baselines (KZ^10 or FL^5), GM-QAOA_3, CBQOA_0, and CBQOA_3, with additional depth comparisons for CBQOA_p where p = 0,1,2,3. The paper reports that CBQOA improves over the classical seed algorithms and outperforms GM-QAOA on the tested hard instances.

## Findings

- [speculative] CBQOA is claimed to apply to a broad class of combinatorial optimization problems, including unconstrained problems and constrained problems such as Max Bisection, Maximum Independent Set, Minimum Vertex Cover, Portfolio Optimization, and Traveling Salesperson, provided the domain satisfies the stated permutation/connectivity assumptions.
- [speculative] A central claimed advantage of CBQOA is that it can solve many constrained problems without adding penalty terms, keeps the quantum evolution within the feasible subspace, and does not require efficient indexing of feasible solutions.
- [supported] For Max 3SAT and Max Bisection, the authors provide simulation-based empirical evidence that CBQOA improves on the classical seed solutions generated by the Karloff-Zwick and Feige-Langberg algorithms, respectively.
- [supported] On the tested hard-instance sets, CBQOA with 3 layers outperformed GM-QAOA with 3 layers in the reported probability-of-good-solutions metric for both Max 3SAT and Max Bisection.
- [supported] The paper reports that even the initialization stage alone (CBQOA with p=0, i.e., CTQW-prepared state without additional alternating layers) can outperform the classical seed generator on the chosen hard instances.
- [supported] For Max Bisection, deeper CBQOA circuits (p = 0, 1, 2, 3) showed progressively better performance in the reported simulations, though with diminishing returns and harder parameter tuning at larger depth.
- [speculative] The authors argue that CBQOA may be more efficient than approaches that search the full bitstring space because its state evolution is restricted to the feasible subspace.
- [speculative] The paper claims the CTQW state-preparation and mixing operators are efficiently implementable with polynomial-depth circuits under the stated locality and graph-construction assumptions.
- [speculative] Any broader implication that CBQOA offers a practical or scalable quantum advantage over classical optimization remains unproven, because results are based on classical simulation of small instances rather than real quantum hardware or asymptotic evidence.

**Results summary:** This preprint introduces CBQOA, a hybrid quantum-classical optimization framework that warm-starts from a classical approximate solution and uses a CTQW-defined feasible-subspace state preparation followed by alternating phase-separation and reflection-like mixing steps. The method is designed to handle both unconstrained and many constrained combinatorial optimization problems without penalty reformulations. The paper demonstrates the construction for Max 3SAT and Max Bisection and evaluates performance on classically simulated small hard-instance benchmarks. On 100 hard Max 3SAT instances (16 variables, 200 clauses) and 100 hard Max Bisection instances (12-vertex random graphs), CBQOA improved over the classical seed algorithms and outperformed GM-QAOA in the paper's probability-of-good-solutions metric, with 3-layer CBQOA performing best among the tested variants. However, these findings are simulation-based and do not demonstrate quantum advantage on hardware.

## Limitations
- The algorithm and all comparisons are evaluated only via classical simulation on very small problem sizes (e.g., Max 3SAT with 16 variables, Max Bisection with 12 vertices), limiting conclusions about scalability to larger, practically relevant instances
- Experiments are performed only on synthetic random instances (random 3SAT formulas and Erdős–Rényi graphs) rather than structured or real-world problem instances
- The CTQW for Max Bisection is implemented approximately using low-order Trotterization with a fixed small Trotter number N=3, introducing uncontrolled approximation error in the state preparation
- The choice of edge weights in the CTQW graph is acknowledged as heuristic (a sigmoid of f(z)−f(τ_i(z))) and potentially suboptimal; no systematic comparison with alternative weighting schemes is provided
- The performance of CBQOA is demonstrated only on two problems (Max 3SAT and Max Bisection); other claimed applications (e.g., Portfolio Optimization, Maximum Independent Set, Traveling Salesperson) are not empirically validated
- The parameter optimization relies on general-purpose classical optimizers (ADAM, L-BFGS-B) and CVaR-based objectives, which may be expensive and difficult to scale as the number of parameters and problem size grow
- The accelerated classical simulation technique for CBQOA relies on a coarse-graining parameter M and assumes |γ_j| = O(1); its practical accuracy–cost trade-off is only analyzed theoretically, not empirically
- The method assumes the existence of efficiently computable O(log n)-local permutations satisfying Assumption 3.1; for some constrained problems (e.g., Knapsack) this does not hold and the approach reverts to penalty methods, losing the feasibility guarantees

## Contradictions
- #contradiction:scalability — While the paper suggests CBQOA could apply to practical problems (including portfolio optimization and other large constrained tasks), all evidence comes from classical simulations on very small synthetic instances (n≈12–16), with no hardware tests or scaling studies, contradicting any strong implication that the method currently scales to realistic financial problem sizes.
- #contradiction:scalability — The work emphasizes confinement to the feasible subspace without penalties for many constrained problems, but explicitly notes that for some important constrained problems (e.g., Knapsack-type), the required local permutations do not exist and the method reverts to penalty encodings, undermining the generality of the claimed feasibility advantage.

## Abstract summary
The paper proposes a hybrid quantum-classical algorithm that integrates quantum computing with full configuration interaction quantum Monte Carlo (FCIQMC) by representing walkers as quantum states prepared by shallow circuits. The authors introduce nonstoquasticity indicators to quantify the sign problem and develop quantum-circuit-based spawning and propagation procedures that remain efficient even when the transformed Hamiltonian is nonsparse. Numerical simulations on N2 and a 2×4 Hubbard model show that this QC-FCIQMC approach substantially mitigates the sign problem and improves ground-state energy accuracy compared to both standard FCIQMC and variational quantum eigensolvers using shallow circuits.
## Methodology
The paper proposes and numerically evaluates a hybrid quantum-classical quantum Monte Carlo method, QC-FCIQMC, that replaces classical FCIQMC walkers with orthogonal quantum-state walkers generated by a variationally optimized unitary circuit U. The workflow is: first run a variational quantum algorithm to obtain a shallow circuit U that prepares an approximate ground state and defines a rotated walker basis |phi_i> = U|i>; then perform full configuration interaction quantum Monte Carlo in that basis using spawning, death/cloning, and annihilation steps governed by the transformed Hamiltonian U^dagger H U. To make propagation feasible despite the transformed Hamiltonian being dense, the authors derive quantum-circuit-based sampling procedures that estimate or sample matrix elements and signs of the transformed Hamiltonian using ancilla-assisted circuits and a Bernoulli-factory square-root step. The study also introduces nonstoquasticity indicators (NSIs) and upper bounds as theoretical measures of sign-problem severity, then validates the method numerically on two many-body benchmarks: the N2 molecule and the 2x4 Hubbard model. Performance is assessed by sign-problem suppression, energy accuracy, energy variance/standard deviation, walker population concentration, and walker-count reduction, with comparisons against classical FCIQMC and standalone variational quantum algorithms such as ADAPT-VQE.

**Algorithms used:** QC-FCIQMC, FCIQMC, VQE, ADAPT-VQE, Hamiltonian variational ansatz

**Experimental setup:** Numerical simulations of a hybrid QC-FCIQMC algorithm were performed. Quantum walkers were prepared by shallow variational circuits and then used within FCIQMC. The paper presents ancilla-assisted quantum circuits for estimating transformed Hamiltonian matrix elements, sampling spawning probabilities, and tracking signs/phases, but the reported results are numerical rather than executions on a real QPU. Numerics were supported by the High-performance Computing Platform of Peking University.

**Dataset:** Synthetic benchmark quantum many-body systems rather than financial datasets: (1) N2 molecule in a minimum STO-3g basis with frozen 1s and 2s orbitals, encoded on 12 qubits; (2) 2x4 half-filled Hubbard model with U/t = 4, encoded on 16 qubits.
## Experiment details
### Input
Two benchmark problem instances were used. For N2, the molecule was represented in the minimum STO-3g basis, with 1s and 2s orbitals frozen, yielding a 12-qubit encoding; experiments included potential-energy-surface calculations across bond lengths and a focused study at bond length 4.0 Å. For the Hubbard benchmark, a 2x4 lattice at half-filling with interaction ratio U/t = 4 was encoded using 16 qubits. No external empirical dataset was used; inputs were Hamiltonians from quantum chemistry and condensed-matter models. The appendix states that figure data and Hamiltonians generated from the VQE basis are publicly available on Zenodo.

### Process
The experimental pipeline was: (1) choose a benchmark Hamiltonian (N2 or Hubbard model); (2) run a variational quantum algorithm to obtain a shallow unitary U defining the walker basis. For N2, ADAPT-VQE circuits of varying depth were used, with examples using 12, 20, and 24 added fermionic operators; for the Hubbard model, Hamiltonian variational ansatz circuits with 1, 5, 10, and 15 layers were used. (3) Initialize QC-FCIQMC with N0 walkers in the variationally prepared reference state |phi_0>; (4) iterate imaginary-time evolution using spawning, death/cloning, and annihilation. Spawning probabilities were based on transformed Hamiltonian elements |H~_ji|Δτ, with |H~_ji| and signs estimated via ancilla-assisted quantum circuits; a Bernoulli-factory square-root procedure was used to sample from |H~_ji| starting from samples proportional to |H~_ji|^2. (5) Start the adaptive energy-shift population-control procedure once the total walker count exceeded 10,000 walkers in the main benchmarks. (6) Estimate energy using the mixed estimator after convergence/stabilization of walker population. Additional studies varied circuit depth, measured NSI upper bounds, tracked energy variance/standard deviation, examined walker population concentration, and tested finite sampling noise by limiting the number of sampled spawned configurations; for N2 at 4.0 Å they report accurate results with millions of samples for the entire QC-FCIQMC process.

### Output
Outputs included potential energy surfaces for N2, ground-state energy estimates, energy differences from exact/FCI values, standard deviation or variance of energy evaluations along QMC evolution, nonstoquasticity indicator (NSI) upper bounds, walker population distributions, and the number of walkers required to achieve a target variance. Baseline comparisons were made against classical single-determinant FCIQMC and standalone variational methods: ADAPT-VQE for N2 and VQE with Hamiltonian variational ansatz for the Hubbard model. Reported findings include chemical-accuracy-level performance for N2 across bond lengths, two-to-three-orders-of-magnitude accuracy improvement over variational quantum algorithms, exponential decrease of sign problem indicators with circuit depth, and substantial reduction in walker counts needed for fixed precision.

**Results summary:** This peer-reviewed empirical paper presents a hybrid quantum-classical QC-FCIQMC algorithm and evaluates it numerically on two benchmark many-body problems: the N2 molecule encoded on 12 qubits and a 2x4 Hubbard model on 16 qubits. The main empirical result is that using shallow-circuit quantum-state walkers substantially mitigates the QMC sign problem compared with classical single-determinant FCIQMC, while also improving energy estimation accuracy beyond standalone variational quantum algorithms. For N2, the method reaches chemical accuracy across bond lengths with about 10,000 walkers and yields much smaller energy variance than classical FCIQMC; at 4.0 Å, variance is reduced by roughly two orders of magnitude when using a deeper ADAPT-VQE-generated basis, and the walker count needed for a fixed variance target drops by about 100x from 4 to 24 operators. For the Hubbard model, the method similarly reduces energy fluctuations and concentrates walker populations as ansatz depth increases. The paper also introduces nonstoquasticity indicators (NSIs) and shows numerically that NSI decreases with circuit depth, correlating with reduced variance and sign-problem severity. All reported evidence is from numerical simulation, not real hardware experiments.

## Quantum advantage claim
**Classification:** speculative

## Limitations
- Numerical validation is limited to small benchmark systems: the N2 molecule on 12 qubits and the Hubbard model on 16 qubits.
- The method still faces exponential scaling in the worst case; the authors explicitly note that the sign problem remains NP-hard.
- Experiments are numerical simulations rather than demonstrations on actual quantum hardware, so hardware noise, decoherence, gate errors, and readout errors are not empirically validated.
- Practical performance depends on finding a good shallow quantum circuit or initial state; the search for an optimal circuit is acknowledged as a separate task and may itself be difficult.
- The approach assumes access to a VQE-optimized or otherwise high-overlap initial state to mitigate the sign problem effectively.
- The sampling procedure introduces shot-noise-induced bias when estimating spawning probabilities; although the authors report it becomes small with sufficient samples, it is not eliminated.
- Resource requirements for real-device deployment are not fully characterized; the paper explicitly says detailed resource requirements, error mitigation strategies, and experimental realization remain to be studied.
- Scalability beyond proof-of-concept instances is not established, despite claims of efficiency and scalability.

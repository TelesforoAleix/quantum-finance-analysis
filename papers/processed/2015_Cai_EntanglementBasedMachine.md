## Abstract summary
The paper experimentally demonstrates entanglement-based quantum machine learning algorithms on a small-scale photonic quantum computer. The authors implement supervised and unsupervised classification of 2-, 4-, and 8-dimensional vectors into clusters by encoding vectors into multi-photon entangled states and estimating distances via single-qubit measurements. This work serves as a proof of principle that quantum processors can naturally perform high-dimensional vector manipulations central to many machine learning tasks, with scaling that is independent of vector dimension in the distance estimation subroutine.
## Methodology
This preprint reports an empirical, proof-of-principle experimental demonstration of entanglement-based quantum machine learning on a small-scale photonic quantum computer. The method follows the Lloyd-Mohseni-Rebentrost quantum classification idea, where high-dimensional classical vectors are amplitude-encoded into quantum states and classification is performed by estimating inner products and Euclidean distances via an ancilla-assisted entangled state. Specifically, the authors prepare 2-, 3-, and 4-photon entangled resource states to represent 2-, 4-, and 8-dimensional vectors, respectively, using polarization-encoded photonic qubits generated by spontaneous parametric down-conversion. A new sample vector and a reference vector are embedded into an entangled state of the form (|0>|u> + |1>|v>)/sqrt(2), and repeated projective measurements on the ancillary photon are used to estimate a success probability p, from which the inner product and distance are computed. The experimental study evaluates supervised binary classification with one reference vector per cluster for 2D, 4D, and 8D vectors, and also demonstrates supervised nearest-neighbor and unsupervised clustering conceptually/experimentally on small toy examples. Performance is assessed by comparing experimentally estimated distance differences and resulting class assignments against theoretical predictions, while also characterizing the quality of the entangled resource states via state fidelity and entanglement witnesses.

**Algorithms used:** Entanglement-based quantum classification, Quantum nearest-neighbor classification, Unsupervised clustering

**Experimental setup:** Experiments were conducted on a real small-scale photonic quantum processor using polarization-encoded single-photon qubits. Ultraviolet laser pulses (394 nm central wavelength, 120 fs pulse duration, 76 MHz repetition rate) pumped type-II BBO crystals to generate entangled photon pairs via spontaneous parametric down-conversion. Two-photon, three-photon, and four-photon entangled states were used as resources for 2D, 4D, and 8D vector classification. Sagnac-like interferometers, PBS/NBS elements, HWPs, prisms, and single-photon detectors were used to encode vectors, interfere paths, and register coincidence events. Measurements were based on repeated projective measurements of the ancillary photon, with coincidence counting used to estimate probabilities.

**Dataset:** No real financial or benchmark dataset was used. The experiments used small synthetic/vector examples: 100 randomly tested 2-dimensional vectors with two randomly chosen reference vectors; 17 selected 4-dimensional test vectors with fixed reference vectors A=(1,0,0,0) and B=(0,0,1,1); 9 selected 8-dimensional test vectors with fixed reference vectors A=(1,0,0,0,0,0,0,0) and B=(0,0,0,0,0,0,0,1); plus toy examples for supervised nearest-neighbor and unsupervised clustering on 2D points.
## Experiment details
### Input
Synthetic low-dimensional vectors encoded as quantum states. For 2D classification, 100 tested vectors were evaluated against two randomly chosen reference vectors v_A=(1.50, 0.55) and v_B=(0.86, 2.35). For 4D classification, 17 manually listed test vectors were compared to reference vectors A=(1,0,0,0) and B=(0,0,1,1). For 8D classification, 9 manually listed test vectors were compared to reference vectors A=(1,0,0,0,0,0,0,0) and B=(0,0,0,0,0,0,0,1). Vectors were normalized and amplitude-encoded into 1, 2, or 3 photonic qubits depending on dimension. No external data source, temporal range, or preprocessing beyond normalization/encoding is reported.

### Process
1. Generate polarization-entangled photon pairs via spontaneous parametric down-conversion. 2. Prepare 2-photon, 3-photon, or 4-photon entangled resource states corresponding to 2D, 4D, or 8D vector encoding. 3. Encode the new sample vector and reference vector(s) into photonic qubit states using PBS-based path splitting and wave-plate-controlled unitary operations in Sagnac-like interferometers. 4. Construct ancilla-vector entangled states of the form (|0>|u> + |1>|v>)/sqrt(2). 5. Perform repeated projective measurements on the ancillary photon and estimate the success probability p from coincidence counts. 6. Compute inner products and Euclidean distances using the analytical relations given in the paper. 7. For supervised classification, compare D_A and D_B and assign the sample to the cluster with smaller distance. 8. For unsupervised clustering, initialize a random grouping, compute each vector's mean distance to each group, reassign to the group with smaller mean distance, and iterate until no labels change. 9. Characterize entangled resource quality using entanglement witnesses and fidelity estimates.

### Output
Outputs include experimentally estimated distance differences (D_A - D_B), cluster assignments, and comparison with theoretical predictions. For 2D classification, results are visualized as a decision map over 100 tested vectors, with 2 misclassifications reported. For 4D and 8D classification, tabulated results list each test vector, theoretical and experimental distance differences, assigned group, and correctness; 1 misclassification is reported in each of the 4D and 8D sets. Additional outputs include entangled-state fidelities of 0.94 (2-photon), 0.73 (3-photon), and 0.75 (4-photon), and qualitative discussion of error sources such as imperfect entanglement, dark counts, double-pair emission, imperfect interference, and phase fluctuations. No classical machine-learning baseline is used beyond comparison to theoretical ideal predictions.

## Quantum advantage claim
**Classification:** speculative

Although the paper experimentally demonstrates the classification subroutine on real photonic hardware, its claims of exponential speedup are theoretical and based on small proof-of-principle instances. No practical or large-scale quantum advantage over classical methods is empirically demonstrated in this preprint.
## Limitations
- Proof-of-principle experiment only on a small-scale photonic quantum processor
- Experiments are limited to classifying only 2-, 4-, and 8-dimensional vectors
- Classification task is restricted to two clusters with one reference vector per cluster (M = 1) in the main demonstration
- The current experimental scheme can in principle achieve exponential speedup with respect to vector dimension N, but not with respect to the number of training samples M
- Misclassifications occur for vectors close to the decision boundary because experimental error becomes comparable to |DA−DB|
- Performance is degraded by imperfect two-photon entanglement and dark counts of the single-photon detectors
- Distance-evaluation precision for 4- and 8-dimensional cases is limited by relatively low multi-photon entangled-state fidelity (~75%)
- Multi-photon state fidelity is reduced mainly by double-pair emission in parametric down-conversion, imperfect interference of independent photons on the PBS, and phase fluctuations in the Sagnac interferometers
- Only a small number of test vectors were evaluated for higher-dimensional cases (17 vectors for 4D and 9 vectors for 8D)
- Data acquisition is slow and measurement-intensive, requiring repeated measurements and minutes per vector in some cases

## Contradictions
- The paper makes speculative quantum speedup claims, but the evidence is limited to tiny synthetic instances on photonic hardware with no classical baseline, contradicting any strong claim of demonstrated practical quantum superiority.
- The paper suggests scalability to larger dimensions and broader ML acceleration, but the experiment only reaches 8-dimensional vectors with declining multi-photon fidelity and slow measurement-intensive acquisition, contradicting strong scaling claims.
- The claimed asymptotic advantage depends on efficient state preparation and data loading, which are not demonstrated; this undermines the end-to-end quantum advantage narrative and creates a classical-vs-quantum contradiction.

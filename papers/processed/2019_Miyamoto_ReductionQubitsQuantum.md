## Abstract summary
The paper proposes a way to reduce the number of qubits required for quantum Monte Carlo algorithms, motivated by high-dimensional financial applications such as credit portfolio risk measurement. Instead of allocating a separate quantum register for each random variable, the authors implement a pseudo-random number generator (specifically PCG) as a quantum circuit and sequentially generate pseudo-random numbers on a single register while accumulating the integrand. They analyze the error–cost trade-off versus classical and prior quantum Monte Carlo methods, give circuit constructions for the PRNG and for a credit risk model (Merton model), and demonstrate the approach on a small multi-dimensional integration using simulation.
## Methodology
This preprint is primarily a theoretical/method-development paper with a small proof-of-concept simulation. It proposes a qubit-reduction variant of quantum Monte Carlo for high-dimensional financial applications by replacing the usual approach of allocating separate quantum registers for each random variable with sequential generation of pseudo-random numbers (PRNs) on a reusable register, followed by quantum amplitude estimation of the average sampled integrand value. The paper formalizes the method for integrands that can be computed sequentially, derives the error decomposition into sampling error and amplitude-estimation error, and analyzes the trade-off between qubit count and circuit depth. It then gives a concrete circuit-level implementation based on the permuted congruential generator (PCG), including constructions for sequence progression and jump-ahead gates, and discusses application to credit risk measurement under the Merton model. As an empirical demonstration, the authors implement a small-scale two-variable trigonometric integration example on a simulator using an LCG-based PRNG and maximum-likelihood amplitude estimation, comparing the simulator estimate to the exact integral and the exact sample-average over the chosen pseudo-random sample points.

**Algorithms used:** Quantum Monte Carlo, Quantum Amplitude Estimation, Maximum-Likelihood Amplitude Estimation, PCG, LCG
**Frameworks:** Qiskit

**Experimental setup:** A proof-of-concept numerical experiment was run on the Qiskit quantum circuit simulator from IBM. ## Experiment details
### Input
Synthetic integration problem: I = (1/theta^Nvar) integral over [0, theta]^Nvar of sin^2(sum_i x_i) dx with theta = pi/6 and Nvar = 2. Pseudo-random inputs were generated by an LCG with parameters a = 11, c = 0, m = 31, seed = 1, yielding 5-bit PRNs with period 30. The experiment used Nsamp = 8 sample points, consuming 16 PRN sequence elements. No preprocessing beyond mapping PRN outputs to grid points ((x + 1/2)/2^r) was described.

### Process
1. Define the 2D integral as a Monte Carlo average over pseudo-randomly selected grid points. 2. Prepare a uniform superposition over sample indices in register Rsamp. 3. Use a jump-ahead PRNG gate to initialize the starting PRN value for each sample subsequence in register RPRN. 4. Sequentially apply a function gate f and PRNG progression gate PPRN so that each PRN value contributes an Ry rotation to an ancilla register Rrot, effectively accumulating the phase corresponding to the sampled integrand argument. 5. After processing both variables, the probability of measuring |1> on Rrot equals the Monte Carlo estimate in Eq. (25). 6. Estimate this probability using the amplitude-estimation-without-phase-estimation approach: construct Q = -AS0A^-1Schi, prepare states Q^m A|0>, and collect measurement counts for multiple powers m_k. 7. Use maximum-likelihood estimation over the likelihood function L_lik(theta_a) to infer theta_a and hence the target amplitude/probability. The settings were M = 8, N_k = 100 measurements per setting, and m_k = 2^k, following Suzuki et al.

### Output
The output was the estimated value of the integral/probability encoded in the ancilla amplitude. Reported values were: exact value of the original integral = 0.074578; exact average of integrand values on the selected sample points = 0.078394; simulator-based quantum estimate = 0.078391. The comparison baseline was therefore not a classical optimization benchmark but agreement between the quantum estimate and the exact sample-average, plus reference to the exact continuous integral to show discretization/sampling error.

**Results summary:** This preprint proposes a qubit-reduction strategy for quantum Monte Carlo by generating pseudo-random numbers sequentially within a quantum circuit and then using amplitude estimation on the resulting sampled integrand values. The main claim is that this can preserve the usual quadratic Monte Carlo speedup in oracle complexity while substantially reducing qubit requirements for high-dimensional financial applications such as credit risk measurement, at the cost of increased circuit depth. The paper gives a concrete PRNG construction based on PCG, analyzes rough resource scaling, outlines a credit-risk circuit under the Merton model, and provides a small simulator-based proof of concept on a 2-variable integral. The only empirical result is the toy simulation, which shows agreement between the quantum estimate and the exact average over the selected pseudo-random sample set; no real-hardware results or empirical financial-scale benchmarks are provided.

## Limitations
- The proposed qubit reduction comes with an explicit trade-off: circuit depth increases, and full qubit reduction may be impractical.
- Circuit depth becomes proportional to the number of random numbers required, which is problematic for very high-dimensional finance applications.
- The approach assumes the integrand can be computed sequentially in the form y1 = f1(x1), yn = fn(yn-1, xn); the authors note not all functions satisfy this assumption.
- The method requires a PRNG for which both stepwise progression and jump-ahead operations can be implemented efficiently as quantum gates.
- The approach estimates Esamp, the average over pseudo-random samples, rather than Etrue directly; this introduces an additional sampling error term relative to exact expectation estimation.
- The paper acknowledges statistical concerns when using consecutive PRN subsequences in high dimensions, especially regarding homogeneity in high-dimensional spaces.
- The practical usefulness depends on using only a tiny part of a long-period PRNG sequence; this is argued heuristically rather than rigorously validated.
- Without quantum error correction, the deep circuits required by the method are not expected to be executable on near-term quantum hardware.
- Even with error correction, deep circuits may suffer from long runtimes of fault-tolerant gates, limiting practical speed advantages.
- The credit-risk implementation assumes the existence of efficient quantum subroutines for transformations such as distribution conversion and functions like Yi, rather than fully implementing and benchmarking them.
- The demonstration is only a proof of concept on a very small toy integration problem, not on the target financial application of credit risk measurement.
- The simulation uses a very small PRNG setting (5-bit LCG, period 30, 8 samples), and the authors explicitly note statistical concerns from such a small-scale setup.
- No real quantum hardware experiments are reported; validation is limited to simulation.
- The paper does not provide empirical resource estimates for realistic portfolio sizes such as O(10^6) obligors.
- As a preprint, the work has not undergone peer review.

## Contradictions
- The paper claims preserved quadratic quantum Monte Carlo advantage and applicability to large-scale credit risk, but simultaneously acknowledges that circuit depth grows with the number of generated random numbers, making realistic high-dimensional instances impractical on current or near-term hardware.
- The work presents the method as qubit-efficient for very large financial problems, yet provides only simulator-based validation on a tiny 2-variable toy integral with a 5-bit LCG and no empirical benchmark on realistic credit-risk portfolios.

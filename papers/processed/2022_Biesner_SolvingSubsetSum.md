## Abstract summary
This preprint explores the application of binary optimization techniques, specifically quadratic unconstrained binary optimization (QUBO), to solve the subset sum problem in financial auditing and data analysis. The authors reformulate the problem using Hopfield networks and gradient descent, demonstrating its effectiveness on both synthetic and real-world financial datasets. The work also discusses potential advancements using specialized hardware and quantum algorithms for improved scalability.
## Methodology
The paper addresses the subset sum problem in the context of automated auditing and financial data analysis by reformulating it as a Quadratic Unconstrained Binary Optimization (QUBO) problem. The authors employ gradient descent on Hopfield Networks to solve the QUBO problem, which is equivalent to the subset sum problem. The methodology involves converting the subset sum problem into a QUBO formulation and then leveraging the energy minimization properties of Hopfield Networks to find solutions. The approach is evaluated on both artificial and real financial datasets, where the artificial data is generated by uniformly sampling integers within specified ranges, and the real data is extracted from financial reports containing quarterly financial statements. The algorithm's performance is measured in terms of computation time and the number of runs required to find a solution.

**Algorithms used:** Hopfield Networks, Gradient Descent

**Experimental setup:** Experiments were conducted using an NVIDIA A100 GPU to parallelize the optimization of multiple independent Hopfield Networks. The algorithm was run for up to 1e+8 initializations in batches of 1e+4, with a maximum computation time of approximately 8 minutes per configuration. The setup focused on evaluating the algorithm's reliability and efficiency in solving subset sum problems of varying sizes and magnitudes.

**Dataset:** The study utilized two types of datasets: (1) Artificial data generated by uniformly sampling integers between specified ranges (e.g., [-Xmax, Xmax]) with configurations varying in size (n) and magnitude (Xmax), and (2) Real financial data parsed from a Deutsche Bank financial report containing 190 independent columns of financial statements for quarters Q1 2019 to Q4 2020. The real data included values up to billions of euros, precise to one cent.

**Results summary:** The preprint demonstrates that gradient descent on Hopfield Networks is a reliable method for solving the subset sum problem, a critical task in automated auditing and financial data consistency checks. The algorithm achieved 100% accuracy on both artificial datasets (up to n=128, Xmax=1M) and real-world financial data (190 columns from Deutsche Bank reports). Performance benchmarks showed computation time depends more on the magnitude of numbers than problem size, with mean times ranging from 0.3s (n=128, Xmax=10k) to 77.2s (n=32, Xmax=1M).

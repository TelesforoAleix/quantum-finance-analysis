## Abstract summary
This paper introduces an extrapolation method to optimize parameters for the linear-ramp quantum approximate optimization algorithm (QAOA), aiming to improve its scalability for combinatorial optimization problems. The authors propose a technique to estimate optimal QAOA parameters for larger problem sizes by extrapolating from smaller instances, reducing the computational overhead of parameter optimization. The method is applied to financial and machine learning use cases, such as portfolio optimization, to evaluate quantum runtime scaling and compare it with classical approaches.
## Methodology
The paper presents an empirical study evaluating the runtime scaling of the linear-ramp quantum approximate optimization algorithm (LR-QAOA) for solving combinatorial optimization problems in financial services, such as portfolio optimization, feature selection, clustering, and weighted MaxCut. The research design involves a two-part approach: classical optimization and quantum optimization. For classical optimization, the authors benchmark several classical solvers (CPLEX, GUROBI, MQLib/Burer2002 Heuristic, and Goemans-Williamson algorithm) on quadratic unconstrained binary optimization (QUBO) problems derived from financial datasets. The runtime scaling of these solvers is evaluated as a function of the number of variables (bits). For quantum optimization, the authors propose an extrapolation method to estimate suitable QAOA parameters (Δγ, Δβ, and depth p) for larger problem sizes based on optimizations performed on smaller subproblems. The linear-ramp QAOA schedule is used to reduce the number of free parameters to two, enabling efficient parameter optimization. The quantum runtime scaling is evaluated using a noiseless quantum emulator, and the results are compared with classical runtime scaling to assess potential quantum advantages.

**Algorithms used:** QAOA, Linear-Ramp QAOA
**Frameworks:** Qiskit

**Experimental setup:** Experiments were conducted using a noiseless quantum emulator. The quantum circuits were executed with varying depths (p) and parameters (Δγ, Δβ) extrapolated from smaller subproblems (N = 4, 6, 8, 10) to larger problem sizes (up to N = 28 qubits). The classical optimization benchmarks were run on standard computational hardware, with runtime measurements repeated multiple times to account for variability.

**Dataset:** The datasets used include: (1) Portfolio optimization: Annualized returns and covariance matrices for the German DAX 30 (2016-2020), with problem instances generated by randomly selecting N out of 30 assets. (2) Feature selection: 'German Credit Data' with 20 features (7 numerical, 13 categorical), expanded to 48 binary features after one-hot encoding. (3) Clustering: 'Moons' and 'Blobs' datasets from SCIKIT-LEARN with two features. (4) Weighted MaxCut: Randomly weighted graphs with edge weights drawn from a uniform discrete distribution between 0 and 1000.
